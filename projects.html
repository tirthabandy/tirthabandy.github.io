Autonomy for Mobility on Demand (MoD)

As the use of private vehicles starts approaching its limits to effectively meet the demand for personal mobility in densely populated cities, mobility-on-demand systems emerge as a more economical and sustainable alternative. One of the main challenges in managing mobility-on-demand systems is in keeping a balanced distribution of the vehicles among different stations to ensure minimal waiting time for the customers at sustainable cost. We explore the use of vehicle autonomy in addressing this problem.

Our research deals with developing an low cost autonomous testbed using off-the-shelf sensors able to navigate in a crowded urban environment in the presence of other vehicles and pedestrians on the road. The system developed also acts as a test bed for testing various technologies developed at FM for improving personal mobility in an urban setting. Read more...

Intention-Aware Motion Planning

As robots venture into new application domains as autonomous vehicles on the road or as domestic helpers at home, they must recognize human intentions and behaviors in order to operate effectively. This generates a new class of motion planning problems with uncertainty in human intention. Lack of an intention sensor makes the problem challenging with the need to infer the human intentions from noisy sensory data, limited observation history and temporary occlusions.

We integrate the perception of the environment and human intentions with planning under such uncertainty in a unified POMDP framework. As an example we show the intention aware planning in the context of pedestrians avoidance on the road. Read more...

Robust Navigation in Marine Environment

Our objective is to collect oceanographic and environmental information using autonomous robotic systems in Singapore waters. The data can be used for environmental monitoring of toxins like algal blooms, or industrial wastes or for surveillance and inspection of marine structures. Unknown obstacles and moving traffic pose a major concern in autonomous operations. The large number of ships, numerous smaller boats and sudden appearance of fishing platforms (kelongs) in Singapore waters, prevent planning a collision-free motion trajectory a-priori.

We propose a safe navigation scheme for a single and multiple Autonomous Surface Vehicles (ASVs), that use the information from onboard sensors to plan for a collision free path in real-time while performing their data collection activities. Read more...

Navigation in Crowded and Dynamic Environments

Our motivation is to build autonomous robots that can follow people and recognize their activities. Such capabilities are important in applications such as home care for the elderly, intelligent environments, and iteractive media. The challenges exist when the robot has to operate in environments which are extremely crowded leading to firstly, significant occlusions to features relevant for self-localization and secondly, dynamic changes to the free space available for the robot to navigate.

In particular, the goal of this project is to develop reliable and efficient motion strategies for an autonomous robot to follow a target and keep it within sensing range. In this research, we investigate the use of greedy strategies using local information to acheive the goal and show the performance in a crowded environment (our school cafeteria at lunch time). Target Following in Unknown Environments

Keeping the target in view in an unpredictable environment, the robot must balance between the short-term goal of preventing the immediate loss of the target and the long-term goal of keeping it visible for the maximum duration possible. Interestingly, a good comprise can be achieved, using on local information available to the robot’s visual sensors.

We propose a local greedy approach to target following the current geometric relationships between the robot, the target, and the localy sensed environment both in 2-D and 3-D. Interesting behaviors are achieved by adding additional mission constraints like stealth. Read more...
